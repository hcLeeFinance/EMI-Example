The Asset Allocation Problem

How should we split our capital?

Suppose you have $10,000 USD. You want to allocate it between two different assets:

Asset A High-Growth Stock

Expected Return ($R_A$): 12%

Volatility ($\sigma_A$): 20%

Return / Risk: 0.60

• High growth potential, but high uncertainty.

Asset B Stable Utility Stock

Expected Return ($R_B$): 6%

Volatility ($\sigma_B$): 8%

Return / Risk: 0.75

• Steady dividend income, highly predictable.

What are the optimal weights ($w_A, w_B$)?

  • Should we invest 100% in Asset A for the highest return?
  • Should we use a simple 50/50 split (Equal Weight)?
  • How much return do we earn per one unit of risk?
  • Is there a mathematical approach to optimize our weights?
Step 1 of 10

Define Asset Return & Risk

Risk is measured by Volatility

In finance, expected return is always coupled with risk. We measure risk using Standard Deviation ($\sigma$, Sigma), also called Volatility.

Asset A High Risk

Expected Return ($R_A$): 12%

Volatility ($\sigma_A$): 20%

Prices swing wildly. High growth but high chance of temporary loss.

Asset B Low Risk

Expected Return ($R_B$): 6%

Volatility ($\sigma_B$): 8%

Prices are very steady. Low swings, highly stable capital preservation.

Step 2 of 10

Measure Asset Correlation

The magic behind risk reduction

Correlation Coefficient ($\rho$, Rho) measures how the two assets move together, ranging from $-1.0$ to $+1.0$:

  • 🔗 $\rho = +1.0$ (Perfect Positive): They move together in the exact same direction. No diversification benefits.
  • 🔗 $\rho = 0.0$ (Uncorrelated): They do not move together in a linear way. Good risk reduction.
  • 🔗 $\rho = -1.0$ (Perfect Negative): They move in exact opposite directions. Complete risk cancellation!

By finding assets with low or negative correlation, we can reduce our total portfolio risk without sacrificing expected return. This is the heart of Modern Portfolio Theory.

Step 3 of 10

Calculate Portfolio Return & Risk

Combining assets changes both return and risk

Let $w_A$ and $w_B$ be the weights of Asset A and Asset B, where $w_A + w_B = 1$.

Portfolio Expected Return ($E(R_p)$)

Calculated as a simple, linear weighted average of individual returns:

$$E(R_p) = w_A \times R_A + w_B \times R_B$$

The higher the weight on the high-return asset, the higher the portfolio return.

Portfolio Risk ($\sigma_p$)

Non-linear combination due to asset correlation $\rho$:

$$\sigma_p = \sqrt{w_A^2\sigma_A^2 + w_B^2\sigma_B^2 + 2w_Aw_B\sigma_A\sigma_B\rho}$$

💡 If $\rho < 1.0$, $\sigma_p$ is strictly lower than the simple weighted average of individual risks — this is the diversification benefit!

Step 4 of 10

The Sharpe Ratio — Measuring Risk-Adjusted Performance

How much excess return do we earn per unit of risk?

Sharpe Ratio ($S_p$)
$$S_p = \frac{\overbrace{E(R_p) - R_f}^{\text{excess return above risk-free rate}}}{\underbrace{\sigma_p}_{\text{portfolio volatility}}}$$
How to Interpret
  • A higher Sharpe Ratio means better performance per unit of risk taken
  • Allows fair comparison across portfolios with different return and risk levels
  • A Sharpe of 0.5: for every 1% of risk taken, earn 0.5% above the risk-free rate
Numerical Example

If $R_f = 2\%$, $E(R_p) = 9\%$, $\sigma_p = 11.49\%$:

$$S_p = \frac{9\% - 2\%}{11.49\%} \approx 0.61$$

This portfolio earns 0.61% of excess return per 1% of risk taken.

Step 5 of 10

Optimization Objectives & Constraints

What exactly are MVP and Tangency Portfolio solving?

Minimum Variance Portfolio (MVP)

Goal: find the lowest possible portfolio risk.

$$\min_{w_A,w_B}\ \sigma_p^2$$ $$\text{s.t.}\quad w_A + w_B = 1,\quad 0 \leq w_A,w_B \leq 1$$

This portfolio does not directly maximize return. It focuses on the minimum risk point.

Tangency Portfolio (Max Sharpe)

Goal: find the best excess return per unit of risk.

$$\max_{w_A,w_B}\ \frac{E(R_p)-R_f}{\sigma_p}$$ $$\text{s.t.}\quad w_A + w_B = 1,\quad 0 \leq w_A,w_B \leq 1$$

This portfolio balances return and risk through the Sharpe Ratio.

Step 6 of 10

Closed-Form Solution: MVP

Minimum Variance Portfolio weight

Minimum Variance Portfolio

This formula finds the Asset A weight that minimizes portfolio variance.

$$w_A^* = \frac{\sigma_B^2 - \sigma_A \times \sigma_B \times \rho}{\sigma_A^2 + \sigma_B^2 - 2 \times \sigma_A \times \sigma_B \times \rho}$$

The weight of Asset B is $w_B^* = 1 - w_A^*$.

Step 7 of 10

Closed-Form Solution: Tangency

Maximum Sharpe Ratio portfolio weight

Tangency Portfolio

This formula uses excess return because Sharpe Ratio measures return above the risk-free rate.

$$w_A^{\text{tan}} = \frac{(R_A - R_f) \times \sigma_B^2 - (R_B - R_f) \times \sigma_A \times \sigma_B \times \rho} {(R_A - R_f) \times \sigma_B^2 + (R_B - R_f) \times \sigma_A^2 - [(R_A - R_f) + (R_B - R_f)] \times \sigma_A \times \sigma_B \times \rho}$$

Here, $R_A - R_f$ and $R_B - R_f$ are excess returns.
